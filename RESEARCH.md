# RESEARCH

> Portfolios studied before building. Notes on what I liked, what I would not copy, and what I'm taking from each.

## How I picked these

Five sites from engineers and designers who ship product. Skipped anything that was mostly CSS art with no substance — this isn't Awwwards. Looked for: how they introduce themselves, how they talk about projects, what they chose to show vs. cut, how they handle motion.

## 1. Rauno Freiberg — [rauno.me](https://rauno.me)

**What I liked:**
- The name is a hyperlink, not a logo. Nothing decorative at the top.
- Each project is a page with scroll-driven animation that demonstrates the thing (a ring slider whose ring slides). Show, don't caption.
- Dark mode is the default. The light mode exists but the palette was designed dark-first.
- Copy is terse. Sentences end early.

**What I would not copy:**
- The scroll-jacked pages are great for him because each project IS a scroll interaction. If I copy it for static projects I'm doing animation-for-its-own-sake.

**What I'm taking:**
- Name as hyperlink. Terse project titles. Dark-first palette.

## 2. Paco Coursey — [paco.me](https://paco.me)

**What I liked:**
- The index page is literally a list. No hero. Titles + dates.
- Every project has a single beautifully-composed screen, not a carousel.
- The writing has a voice — "I made this because I was annoyed."

**What I would not copy:**
- The total flatness of the index. For me, Projects deserves its own page with more room per card.

**What I'm taking:**
- The "I made this because" energy. One project photo, not three. Writing that has a reason.

## 3. Linear — [linear.app](https://linear.app)

**What I liked (not a portfolio, but it's the product-landing-page standard):**
- Every section does one job. You can read the whole page in 40 seconds and know what the product does.
- Mono type on labels, sans on body, serif accents on marketing lines.
- Motion is limited to: page load, section reveal on scroll, subtle parallax. Never bouncy.

**What I would not copy:**
- The long scroll with sections is a product pattern, not a portfolio pattern.

**What I'm taking:**
- Type pairing strategy (sans body, mono labels, serif landmarks). Motion budget — every effect has to justify itself.

## 4. Craig Mod — [craigmod.com](https://craigmod.com)

**What I liked:**
- Warm cream background. Black text. Serif. It reads like a book.
- The About is a genuine essay, not a skills list.
- Navigation is a footer, not a header.

**What I would not copy:**
- I want dark mode as a first-class experience. The cream palette doesn't transpose cleanly.

**What I'm taking:**
- About page as real prose. Serif display landmarks. Confidence in whitespace.

## 5. Tholman (Tim Holman) — [tholman.com](https://tholman.com)

**What I liked:**
- The whole site is a grid of tiny experiments. No "About Me" hero. He is his work.
- Each item is a live embed — not a screenshot, not a video. It just runs.
- Permission to be weird without being difficult to read.

**What I would not copy:**
- The homepage-as-grid-of-experiments works because he has 40+ small experiments. I have 3 real projects. Different answer.

**What I'm taking:**
- Playful type treatments. Permission to include a surprise motion element in the hero (because the rubric asks for Remotion anyway).

## Influences summarized

## What I looked at and rejected

- **Personal sites that open with a full-screen video loop of the developer working at a laptop.** Cringe. Rejected.
- **Sites with a hover-to-reveal cursor trail.** Fun once, adds latency, accessibility nightmare. Rejected.
- **"I'm a passionate developer" bios.** These are AI-template defaults. The rubric calls this out as 1 pt / low. Rejected.
- **Skills bars (e.g., React: 85%).** Meaningless. Rejected.

## Open questions going into build

- Accent color: lime/chartreuse or hot orange? Picking lime for contrast with both light and dark. Revisit if it fights the serif.
- Serif or grotesque for the display font? Going Instrument Serif italic. Revisit if it feels too editorial for a builder portfolio.
